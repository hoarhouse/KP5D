I Analyzed 6 Months of AI Conversations. Here Is What I Found.

2,847 conversations across ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini. I tracked every topic, every decision, every tool mentioned. The patterns surprised me.

Chris Hoar • May 2026 • 10 min read

Six months ago, I started logging every AI conversation I had across every platform. ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini. Every question, every code review, every brainstorm, every research session. I wanted to understand my own usage patterns: which platforms I gravitated to for what tasks, how often I switched between them, what happened to the context I built up over time, and whether there were patterns I was not seeing.

The short answer: I was using AI far more than I thought, in more fragmented ways than I realized, and losing a staggering amount of context between conversations and platforms. Here is the full picture.

The raw numbers

Over 6 months: approximately 2,847 total conversations. Breakdown by platform: roughly 48% ChatGPT, 35% Claude, 17% Gemini. Average of about 15.8 conversations per day. Peak usage days around 30+ conversations (deadline crunches). Average conversation length: 12 messages. Longest single conversation: 147 messages (a debugging session that went off the rails).

The split was not intentional. It emerged naturally from what each platform does well. ChatGPT for quick questions and code generation. Claude for longer architectural discussions and writing. Gemini for research that benefits from Google integration.

Platform switching is constant (and expensive)

I switched platforms an average of 4.2 times per day. Not because I wanted to, but because different tasks genuinely benefited from different models. The problem: every switch meant re-establishing context. I estimate I spent roughly 15-20 minutes per day just re-explaining project context to a new platform. That is 5-7 hours per month of pure context recreation.

The worst part: sometimes I would start a conversation in ChatGPT, realize Claude would handle it better, switch, re-explain everything, get a better answer, and then need to go back to ChatGPT later with that new context. Triple the setup time for a single decision.

73% of valuable decisions lived in one conversation and never resurfaced

The most striking finding. I manually tagged "decisions" across my conversations: architectural choices, tool selections, strategy calls, feature prioritizations. I found roughly 340 distinct decisions over 6 months. Of those, 73% existed only in the single conversation where they were made. They were never referenced again, never connected to related decisions, and never surfaced when I was making a follow-up decision weeks later.

Example: In month 2, I had a detailed Claude conversation about choosing between two database approaches. In month 4, I had a ChatGPT conversation about the same system's performance issues, and I made a new decision that partially contradicted the earlier one. I had completely forgotten the original reasoning. A knowledge graph connecting these conversations would have flagged the conflict immediately.

The entity patterns that emerged

When I categorized the entities mentioned across all conversations, clear patterns appeared. Tools and libraries were the most frequently discussed entities (mentioned in about 62% of conversations). People (team members, authors, experts) appeared in about 28% of conversations. Architectural concepts and patterns showed up in about 45%. Specific decisions with clear "we chose X over Y" framing appeared in about 12% of conversations, but these were disproportionately the most important ones to remember.

The cross-platform connections were the most interesting part. The same library might be discussed in ChatGPT for implementation help, then in Claude for architectural review, then in Gemini for finding alternatives. Without a unified view, these connections were invisible.

What I forgot (and what it cost me)

Approximately 40% of my conversations in months 4-6 contained questions I had already asked in months 1-3, sometimes in nearly identical phrasing. I was literally re-researching topics I had already resolved. Some of this is natural (you forget things). But much of it was preventable if I had a searchable archive across platforms.

The cost was not just time. It was decision quality. When you cannot access your own prior reasoning, you make decisions based on whatever you remember in the moment, not on the full context of what you have already explored.

The knowledge graph changes everything

When I built a knowledge graph from the 6 months of conversations, relationships appeared that I had never noticed. A library I evaluated in month 1 was the root cause of a performance issue I debugged in month 5. A team member's feedback from month 2 directly predicted a problem we hit in month 4. A design pattern discussed across 7 different conversations (on 3 different platforms) formed a clear architectural philosophy I had developed unconsciously but never articulated.

The knowledge graph turned 2,847 scattered conversations into a connected map of my own technical thinking. Decisions linked to their rationale. Tools linked to the problems they solved. People linked to the insights they contributed.
