Kept vs Echoes vs ChatGPT Exporter: AI Conversation Tools Compared

Chris Hoar • March 2026 • 9 min read

The market for AI conversation tools is growing fast. If you search the Chrome Web Store for "ChatGPT export" or "AI conversation manager" you will find dozens of extensions.

I have tested most of them. They broadly fall into three categories: export tools, conversation managers, and cross-platform management interfaces. Each solves a different problem at a different level of depth.

This is an honest comparison of the three most relevant tools in the space right now. I built one of them, so I will be upfront about my bias. But I have also been a daily user of the other two, and I will give credit where it is earned.

Export tools: ChatGPT Exporter, ExportGPT, AI Exporter

These do one thing well. They get your conversations out of the platform and into a file. PDF, Markdown, JSON, sometimes Notion sync. They install in seconds, they are free or cheap, and they work reliably.

ChatGPT Exporter has a 4.8 star rating on the Chrome Web Store for good reason. It is simple and it delivers.

The limitation is clear: export is a one-time action, not a workflow. You get a file, but you cannot search across your exported files. You cannot connect ideas between conversations. Every export is manual and one conversation at a time. You also lose the ability to go back and export again once a conversation is updated.

Best for: occasional backups, sharing a specific conversation with a colleague, one-time archives.

Not for: daily workflows, cross-platform search, building a knowledge base.

Echoes by R2bits: the conversation manager

Echoes deserves real respect. It is the strongest player in the conversation management category and it has earned its position.

Here is what Echoes does well. Cross-platform search across ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, and several other platforms. The search is genuinely good with fuzzy matching, logical operators, and stemming. Labels and favorites for organizing conversations. Conversation summarization. Multi-account management. Cross-device sync.

An active community with 850+ members on Discord. It is a Featured extension on the Chrome Web Store, which tells you Google has vetted it. The free tier is generous and the premium pricing ($5.99 to $49.99) is reasonable for what you get.

I used Echoes before building Kept and I still think it is the right tool for a lot of people. If your primary need is finding and organizing your AI conversations, Echoes handles roughly 70% of what most people need.

The limitation is that Echoes is a browser-only management tool. It helps you find and organize conversations, but it does not offer CLI integration for developer workflows. No file watching for project context. No automated context templating. No ability to fork conversations between models. It manages what you have but does not enhance how you work with AI.

Best for: users who need better search and organization within existing platforms.

Not for: developers who need CLI integration, context templating, or the ability to fork conversations between models.

Kept: the cross-platform AI conversation manager

Full disclosure: I am building Kept, so take this section with appropriate skepticism. I will focus on what Kept does differently rather than claiming it is better at everything.

Kept takes a different approach. Instead of just managing conversations in the browser, it gives you a desktop client, Chrome extension, and CLI that work together. The extension archives conversations from every provider. The desktop client lets you read, search, and connect them. Conversation Forks let you continue any chat in a different model with full context carried over. Context Templating keeps your project files synced with LLM sessions through the CLI. And Dynamic Links connect information across separate chats into a knowledge graph.

Kept offers three capabilities no other tool in this category has:

Conversation Forks: continue any chat in a different model with full context. If you have been architecting a system in Claude and want ChatGPT's take, Kept packages the relevant context and starts the new conversation with background loaded.

Context Templating: define project context once and the CLI keeps it synced. File watcher monitors changes so the agent always sees current state. Tunable retrieval size and context compression make it work even with smaller context windows.

Dynamic Links: connect information across separate chats into a knowledge graph. A decision you made in ChatGPT three months ago gets linked to the library you discussed in Claude last week.

The trade-off is real. Kept is a desktop app (Tauri 2, about 5MB) plus Chrome extension plus CLI, not just a lightweight browser add-on. It requires installation. In return, all your data stays local on your machine, searches are instant through SQLite FTS5, and you own everything. It is MIT licensed and open source.

Best for: developers who use multiple AI platforms daily and want one interface to archive, fork, and template their conversations across every provider.

Not for: users who want a zero-install browser-only solution.

Which one should you use?

Be direct and honest.

If you just need to export a conversation once in a while, use ChatGPT Exporter. It is free, fast, and does the job.

If you want better search and organization across your AI platforms and you want a lightweight browser extension, use Echoes. It is polished, well-maintained, and the community is active.

If you use multiple AI platforms daily and you want structured knowledge extraction, context portability between LLMs, and a local-first architecture where you own all your data, Kept is built for that specific workflow.

These tools are not mutually exclusive. You could use Echoes for daily organization and Kept for knowledge extraction. The question is what problem is most painful for you right now.

One more thing: the platform comparison

Brief section noting that all three of these tools exist because the platforms themselves will never solve the cross-platform problem. ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini are competitors. They are adding memory and search inside their own walls, but they will never index each other.

This structural gap is permanent and it widens with every new model that launches. Third-party tools are not optional. They are infrastructure.

Frequently asked questions

What is the best alternative to Echoes for AI conversations?
Kept is the closest alternative to Echoes with a different approach. Echoes focuses on conversation management (search, labels, organization). Kept provides a desktop client, Chrome extension, and CLI that enable Conversation Forks, Context Templating, and Dynamic Links. Both offer cross-platform search across ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini. The right choice depends on whether you need better organization or a unified interface with CLI integration.
Is there an alternative to ChatGPT Exporter that works across platforms?
ChatGPT Exporter only handles ChatGPT conversations. For cross-platform export and search, Echoes covers multiple platforms with search and labeling. Kept captures from ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini automatically and adds Conversation Forks, Context Templating through CLI, and Dynamic Links on top of search.
Can I search across ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini with one tool?
Yes. Both Echoes and Kept offer cross-platform search. Echoes works as a Chrome extension with fuzzy matching and logical operators. Kept is a desktop app plus Chrome extension plus CLI using SQLite FTS5 for local full-text search. Both index conversations from multiple AI platforms.
What is the difference between an AI conversation manager and a cross-platform management interface?
A conversation manager helps you find, organize, and label your AI conversations within a browser extension. A cross-platform management interface goes further with a desktop client, browser extension, and CLI that work together. Kept offers Conversation Forks to continue chats between models, Context Templating to sync project files with LLM sessions, and Dynamic Links to connect information across separate chats. Echoes is a conversation manager. Kept is a cross-platform management interface.
Is Kept open source?
Yes. Kept is MIT licensed. The core features including Chat Archival, Conversation Forks, Context Templating, and Dynamic Links are free and open source. A premium tier with advanced features is planned to sustain development.
