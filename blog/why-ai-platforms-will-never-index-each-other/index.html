Why AI Platforms Will Never Index Each Other (And What That Means for Your Workflow)

ChatGPT will never search your Claude conversations. Claude will never index your Gemini chats. This structural reality gets worse as more AI platforms launch.

Chris Hoar • March 2026 • 10 min read

Last week I spent 20 minutes trying to find a conversation where I had worked through a database schema decision. I knew I had discussed it with an AI assistant sometime in January. I just could not remember which one.

I opened ChatGPT and searched. Nothing. Opened Claude and searched. Nothing. Opened Gemini. Nothing there either. Turns out I had the conversation in DeepSeek, which does not even have a search feature worth mentioning.

Four platforms. Four separate searches. And I still almost missed it.

This is not a story about bad search features. ChatGPT's search works fine for ChatGPT conversations. Claude's conversation search is solid for Claude conversations. The problem is structural. These are competing companies, and they will never, ever index each other's data.

The email client problem, all over again

Gmail does not show your Outlook emails. Outlook does not show your Gmail messages. That structural gap created a $2 billion market for third-party email clients like Spark, Superhuman, and Apple Mail. The same gap now exists for AI conversations, except the fragmentation is worse because the number of AI platforms is growing faster than email providers ever did.

The email client analogy is the cleanest way to understand this problem. Gmail launched in 2004. Outlook has been around since the 90s. After 20+ years, they still do not index each other. This is not a technical limitation. It is a business decision.

Third-party email clients exist specifically to solve this. Spark offers a unified inbox across Gmail, Outlook, iCloud, Yahoo, and custom IMAP providers. Superhuman works across Gmail and Outlook for $30 per month. They sit above the platforms and unify everything.

The AI conversation space is following the exact same pattern, but faster. In 2024, most developers used one or two AI tools. By 2026, eight major platforms compete for attention: ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, DeepSeek, Grok, Perplexity, Mistral, and Copilot.

Each one stores conversations separately. Each one builds memory features that only work within their own walls. The fragmentation compounds with every new platform.

The email client market took years to develop because email was an established, stable category. The AI conversation client market needs to develop now because the fragmentation is happening in real time.

Every platform is building memory. None of them share it.

ChatGPT launched persistent memory that automatically stores user preferences and conversation context across sessions. Claude added conversation search and memory summaries in 2025. Gemini ties into your Google account for cross-session personalization. Every platform is getting better at remembering you. But each one only remembers conversations you had with them.

ChatGPT memory: Automatically stores user details and preferences across sessions. Creates a user profile that persists. You can ask "What do you remember about me?" and it will show stored information. OpenAI includes this memory at the start of every conversation automatically. With 800 million weekly active users, that's a lot of locked-in memory.

Claude memory: Rolled out in 2025 for Pro/Max/Team/Enterprise users. Uses RAG-based search through actual past conversations. Generates memory summaries every 24 hours. Project-specific memory keeps different workstreams separate. Memory is transparent as visible tool calls like conversation_search and recent_chats.

Gemini memory: Ties into your Google account data, including Search history and Google Workspace activity. The deepest integration with an existing ecosystem. 650 million monthly active users by October 2025, with 44% user growth in Q3 2025. But completely confined to Google's world.

DeepSeek, Grok, Perplexity, Mistral: Various memory implementations, all platform-specific. DeepSeek offers full reasoning capabilities free without usage caps. Grok integrates with X/Twitter data for $30/month SuperGrok. Each builds its own moat.

The pattern is clear: every platform is investing heavily in making their memory better. None of them are investing in making that memory portable.

Each memory system creates a stronger moat. The more ChatGPT knows about you, the harder it is to switch to Claude and start over. This is intentional. As AI Frontiers noted: "Control over user context, not raw model power, will decide who wins the AI commercial race." Platforms know this. That's why they will never share.

Why this is a business problem, not a technical one

There is no technical barrier preventing AI platforms from sharing conversation data. APIs exist. Export formats exist. The barrier is competitive. OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, and every other AI company benefit from keeping your data inside their ecosystem. Sharing would reduce switching costs and weaken the lock-in that drives subscription revenue.

ChatGPT already offers data exports through Settings. Claude lets you download conversation history. The data is not technically trapped. It is strategically trapped.

The technical capability to share exists. MCP (Model Context Protocol) from Anthropic proves that interoperability is possible at the tool level. But conversation data and memory are intentionally excluded from these interoperability efforts.

Platform economics 101: User data creates switching costs. Switching costs create retention. Retention creates revenue. No company voluntarily reduces its own switching costs.

ChatGPT has 800 million weekly active users. That user base and its accumulated memory data is OpenAI's most valuable asset beyond the models themselves. Why would they share that with Claude?

Google's approach with Gemini is the most aggressive. By tying AI memory to your entire Google account (Gmail, Search history, Calendar, Workspace), they make the switching cost exponentially higher. Leaving Gemini means leaving your Google identity.

Anthropic's approach with Claude is more privacy-conscious. Memory is opt-in, transparent tool calls, user-editable summaries. But the conversation data still only lives within Claude.

Even if regulators force data portability (like GDPR's right to data portability), the platforms will comply with bulk exports while keeping the real-time search and memory features confined to their own ecosystem.

Anthropic temporarily cut off Windsurf's access to Claude models when Windsurf was being acquired by OpenAI. Platforms resist interoperability when it threatens competitive position. This is business, not technology.

The gap gets wider, not narrower

In 2024, developers primarily chose between ChatGPT and Claude. By February 2026, eight major platforms compete for developer attention: ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, DeepSeek, Grok, Perplexity, Mistral, and Copilot. Chinese AI firms like Moonshot AI and Alibaba are releasing competitive models monthly. Every new platform creates another silo that your existing tools cannot search.

Count the platforms a typical developer might use in a single week. ChatGPT for quick code generation. Claude for architecture review. Gemini for research in Google Workspace. DeepSeek for a free alternative on complex reasoning. Perplexity for search with citations. Cursor or Copilot for IDE-integrated coding.

JetBrains 2025 report confirms this: 93% of developers use AI tools regularly. 59% use three or more AI tools in parallel. This number is trending upward, not downward.

New platforms launching constantly. Moonshot AI with Kimi K2.5. Alibaba with Qwen3. xAI with Grok. Mistral with Le Chat. The rate of new model releases is accelerating. ChatGPT's market share dropped from 87% to 68% according to Similarweb. Gemini rose from 5.4% to 18.2%.

AI aggregators like Poe, ChatPlayground AI, and Aymo are emerging specifically because developers want multi-model access in one interface. But even aggregators only solve the prompt side. They do not capture, index, or search your conversation history across platforms.

Each new platform you try creates another data silo. The problem compounds linearly with every platform added to your workflow.

In the email world, fragmentation stabilized around 3-4 major providers. In AI, we are heading toward 10+ viable platforms with no sign of consolidation.

What MCP gets right and what it misses

Anthropic's Model Context Protocol (MCP) is the most promising interoperability standard for AI tools. It lets any AI application connect to any MCP-compatible service through a universal adapter, reducing custom integrations. But MCP solves tool interoperability, not conversation portability. Your conversation history, memory, and accumulated context still live inside each platform separately.

MCP standardizes how AI applications request tools, data, and actions from external services. This is genuinely useful. Any MCP client can connect to any MCP server, making models more interchangeable at the tool level.

But MCP operates at the action layer (connecting to GitHub, Google Drive, databases), not the memory layer. Your conversation history is not an MCP resource. Your accumulated AI memory is not exposed through MCP servers.

The AI Frontiers analysis noted that "MCP can liberate context only when a third-party service offers a sufficiently rich API. Because platform owners control those APIs, they have an incentive to constrain what MCP can touch."

Platforms have already shown willingness to restrict interoperability when it threatens their competitive position. MCP is a step forward for tool integration. It is not a solution for conversation portability.

The missing piece remains: a way to search, access, and transfer your actual conversation history across platforms. MCP does not solve this. The platforms will not solve this. That gap is where tools like Kept come in.

The third-party layer is inevitable

Just as email clients like Spark and Superhuman emerged to unify fragmented email providers, a third-party conversation layer will emerge to unify fragmented AI platforms. The only question is what that layer looks like: cloud-based aggregators that route prompts, or local-first tools that capture, index, and own the conversation data on your behalf.

The market is already responding. AI aggregators (Poe, ChatPlayground, Aymo, Fello AI, TypingMind) let you access multiple models from one interface. But they solve prompt routing, not conversation ownership.

The missing piece: a tool that captures your actual conversation history across every platform, indexes it locally, and lets you search, fork, and link conversations regardless of where they originated.

The local-first approach matters here. If your unified conversation layer is another cloud service, you have just created another silo. Kept stores everything on your machine, meaning you own the data regardless of what happens to any AI platform.

The email analogy again: the best email clients work because they sit above the providers and unify everything without replacing them. Kept sits above ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini without trying to replace any of them.

As Fello AI's January 2026 analysis noted: "No single model wins every category. To get the best results, you need a workflow that lets you swap between them." That workflow requires a unifying layer the platforms will never provide.

What this means for your workflow today

If you use multiple AI platforms, your conversation history is already fragmented across every one of them. This fragmentation gets worse every month as you accumulate more conversations in more places. The longer you wait to centralize your archive, the more scattered knowledge you have to recover later.

Accept multi-model as the default. The JetBrains data is clear. The majority of developers use 3+ AI tools. Do not fight this. Use the best model for each task. ChatGPT for brainstorming. Gemini for research in Google Docs. Claude for final editing and precision tasks.

Export regularly. Until you have a unified tool, export your conversation data from each platform periodically. ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini all support data exports. Do not let years of AI conversations accumulate without any backup.

Stop relying on platform memory alone. ChatGPT memory, Claude memory, and Gemini memory are all useful but platform-specific. If you switch platforms, that memory does not follow you. Build your own memory layer.
